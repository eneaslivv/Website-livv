A 40-person professional services firm was paying $34,800 per year in SaaS subscriptions. The visible number looked manageable. The real cost was higher.

One junior operations person spent roughly 30% of their time maintaining integrations and fixing data sync errors between five tools. At $65,000 fully loaded, that added $19,500 to the annual total. Real cost: around $54,300 per year, growing as the stack grew.

This is the pattern that drives the build-vs-buy decision for software. Not one frustrating tool. A compounding cost that only becomes visible when someone maps the whole stack, including the time cost, not just the subscription line items.

The conclusion from years of building custom software for businesses that have hit this threshold is less dramatic than the "rebuild everything" narrative and more expensive than the "add another SaaS layer" advice: most companies that make this decision well replace one or two specific components of their stack, the tools producing the most operational friction, and leave everything else in place.

Two examples from our portfolio that show what the decision looks like in practice:

- **Payper**: A hospitality operator running Shopify for retail and Toast for food service with a Google Sheets reconciliation layer maintained by two staff. We built a unified POS that handled both under one inventory model. Build cost: $60,000 to $80,000. The labor saving alone paid for it inside 15 months.
- **LegalFlow**: A law firm running Clio alongside two spreadsheet workarounds for non-standard billing and compliance logging. The driver was not cost, it was a compliance audit they kept failing. Build cost: $90,000 to $110,000. First year after launch: they passed.

The signals that custom software has crossed into better-value territory:

- Total SaaS stack cost (subscriptions plus integration labor) is approaching $40,000 to $80,000 per year
- The tools were not designed for your vertical, and the mismatch creates measurable staff time
- The SaaS data model conflicts with your actual data structure
- Compliance requirements exceed what the vendor offers at accessible pricing

The TCO math on custom software usually flips at the 24 to 36 month mark for a mid-scale business. A custom system in the example above would cost $80,000 to $120,000 to build, with $8,000 to $12,000 per year in maintenance. Payback window: 18 to 30 months.
