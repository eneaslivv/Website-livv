1/ A 40-person firm's visible SaaS spend: $34,800/year. Real cost after integration labor: $54,300/year. When that gap shows up, custom software starts making economic sense. ↓

2/ The SaaS cost nobody prices in: the time your team spends adapting its processes to fit the tool's assumptions. One junior ops person at 30% of their time on integration maintenance = $19,500/year at $65k fully loaded.

3/ The TCO math on custom software usually flips at the 24-36 month mark. A custom system for the example above: $80k-$120k to build, $8k-$12k/year maintenance. Payback: 18-30 months.

4/ Most companies that get this right replace 1-2 specific tools, not the whole stack. The tools producing the most operational friction go custom. Everything else stays on SaaS.

5/ Two examples from our portfolio:

Payper (hospitality POS replacing Shopify + Toast + Sheets reconciliation): $60k-$80k build, paid back in 15 months.

LegalFlow (case mgmt replacing Clio + 2 spreadsheet workarounds): $90k-$110k build, passed a compliance audit they'd been failing.

6/ The clearest signal that custom is worth evaluating: total SaaS stack cost (subscriptions + integration labor) is approaching $40k-$80k/year, AND the tools were not built for your vertical.
