Most businesses adding AI to their operations pick the wrong workflow first.

They add a chatbot because competitors seem to have one. It answers five questions a month. The integration costs $20k and never pays back. The real problem was not choosing AI — it was skipping the one step that makes AI integration pay: a workflow inventory.

Here is what the sequence actually looks like when it works.

The starting point is a structured review of your twenty most significant workflows. For each one, estimate the annual staff hours spent on it and the error rate. The three or four workflows at the top of that ranking — high volume, high time cost, visible room for improvement — are your integration candidates. The one with the clearest scope and failure condition is where you start. Not the second or third candidate because the first seems harder. The first one.

Once you have a target workflow, there are three patterns for adding AI to it. The first is buying an AI-native tool — products like Klarity for contract intelligence or Intercom Fin for support routing. These make sense when your workflow fits the category the tool was built for and you need to move quickly. The second is connecting an existing application to a model API. Development cost at boutique studio rates runs $8,000 to $25,000 for the initial integration; API usage for a mid-scale business workflow runs $200 to $2,000 per month. The third is building a custom AI agent for workflows that require multi-step reasoning or external tool calls — development runs $30,000 to $120,000 depending on complexity.

The most common way this fails: the proof of concept that never ships. A company runs a successful PoC, the demo is compelling, six months pass, and the integration is still in review. The fix is defining a success criterion before the PoC starts — "if this reduces manual review time on this workflow by 30 percent, we ship it to production within sixty days." That converts a PoC from an open-ended experiment into a ship-or-kill decision.

Key numbers from the piece:

- Adding a single AI feature to an existing app: $8,000–$40,000 in development
- RAG-based knowledge system connecting AI to your document set: $20,000–$80,000
- Custom AI agent for a defined workflow: $30,000–$120,000
- Ongoing API costs for a mid-scale business: $200–$2,000 per month
- Budget 15–25% of initial build cost per year for maintenance
- PoC stage should be capped at four weeks and a defined budget

The prompt engineering by committee failure mode is the one nobody talks about. When five people each have opinions about how the AI should respond and those opinions accumulate in a system prompt, quality degrades. Prompt engineering needs a single responsible owner, not consensus management.
