Most founders ask "should we build or buy?" as a single strategic question for the entire tech stack. That framing produces two predictable failures.

The first is asking too early. A company still discovering the shape of its own operations has no reliable basis for designing a custom system. Custom software built for today's process becomes expensive technical debt when the process changes in eight months, which it will.

The second is asking too broadly. "We should build" applied to the whole stack is a different question from "does this one specific tool produce more friction than it's worth?" The second version is answerable. The first rarely is.

A five-question sequence surfaces the right answer in most cases. What does the workflow cost in staff time per month? Does it fit the tool's built-in assumptions? Who needs to own the data? What does the integration middleware actually cost? And what is the 36-month total cost of ownership once seat growth, add-on modules, and vendor repricing are factored in?

That last question is where most analysis breaks down. The pricing page shows one number. The 36-month reality is different. A 20-person company on a project management SaaS at $15 per seat can find itself paying $28,000-$35,000 over three years on a product that looked like a $13,000 commitment at signup. For CRM, ERP, and project management tools at mid-market scale, 36-month TCO typically runs two to four times the number on the pricing page.

Key numbers from the framework:

- Integration middleware for 12 tools with 20 active connections: $29,000-$72,000 over 36 months in subscription costs alone, before staff maintenance time
- Custom software for a focused workflow app: $30,000-$100,000 to build, $1,000-$3,000/month ongoing
- Teams under 15 people are almost always better positioned on SaaS, even with friction
- The math shifts at 50+ seats, where SaaS seat-count pricing often exceeds the custom alternative
- Budget floor for custom: $20,000-$30,000 at a boutique studio in 2026

The businesses that handle this well don't replace their whole stack. They identify one high-friction tool, run the TCO math on that specific tool, and build only what clears the bar. A targeted custom app takes 12 to 16 weeks and limits the downside significantly compared to a wholesale migration.
